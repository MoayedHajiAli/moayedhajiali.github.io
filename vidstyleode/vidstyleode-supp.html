2. Video Dynamic Interpolation

We interpolate between the motion of two different videos by interpolating between their dynamic representation, resulting in a new, intermediate motion that averages the source and target motion. These results demonstrate that the dynamic latent representation learned by VidStyleODE is rich and well-formed.
In each example, we fix the subject by fixing the content frame and demonstrate the motion of the subject following the dynamics of video A, video B, and a combination between the two at different levels.


  • (0:02) The distance between the legs gradually increases as we interpolate between the dynamics
  • (0:05) The step taken by the right leg smoothly decreases as we go from video A (with a full step) to video B (with a still leg)
  • (0:08) The right arm raises less as we move from video A with a full raise of the arm to video B with a still arm
  • (0:11) The distance between the two legs, the distance between the right arm and the body, and the rotation of the right leg gradually change
  • (0:14) The degree of the rotation of the left leg changes smoothly during the interpolation

3. Local Dynamic Editting

The dynamic latent representation learned by VidStyleODE has spatial correspondence with the motion of local body parts. This allows for editing the motion of specific body parts using the motion from a reference video. We show multiple examples that demonstrate this unique capability of our model.
In each example, we show the motion of the source video in Blue labeled as Co-Driving video 1 , and the reference video in Red labeled as Co-Driving video 2. We also show the results of local blending between the dynamic representation of the two videos. Blue boxes illustrates the motion taken from Co-Driving video 1 and Red boxes corresponds to motion taken from Co-Driving video 2.
In each example, we show the motion of both videos and the results of different blending operations.


  • (0:02) The right arm raise in Video B is coppied to Video A through the blending operation of the spatially-corresponding location in the dynamic latent represenation
  • (0:05) VidStyleODE enables localized motion editing such as editing the swing of the right arm (third column) or the right arm and leg movement (fourth column)
  • (0:08) VidStyleODE copies the leg and arm movement from Video B to Video A
  • (0:11) Our model enables different localized motion editing such as legs movement (third column), hand's movement(fourth column), or right-body movement (fifth column)
  • (0:14) Similar to the last example, VidStyleODE enables fine-grained motion editting

Learning a dynamic motion representation, encoded with a latent ODE, we can extrapolate and interpolate between frames. These results show the capability of VidStyleODE in interpolating between two frames and extrapolating from a single or two frames.
For each example, we label the input frames in blue and the extrapolated/interpolated frames in red. We also show the final output video.

5. Text-Guided Apperance Manipulation

We adapt VidStyleODE for the task of video manipulation by conditioning the video frame generation on an external style code, as described in the main paper. These results demonstrate the capability of our model to perform accurate and diverse manipulation, while producing temporally smooth and consistent videos.


  • (0:03) Our model can manipulate color accurately, and perform fundamental structural manipulation such as changing the jacket into a blouse
  • (0:06) Our model can perform intuitive manipulation the adheres to the target text with minimum change on the clothing style
  • (0:09) Our model performs minimum and localized manipulation (T-shirt or pants color) on the video without affecting text-irrelevant parts
  • (0:12) Our model can manipulate accurately color, sleeve length, and clothing style

We compare our method against the state-of-the-art text-guided video and image manipulation methods.

  • HairCLIP: we considered HairClip for the task of video manipulation by manipulating each frame independently. This shows that although HairCLIP can achieve good manipulation accuracy, it lacks temporal consistency
  • STIT: Being an instance-optimization method, STIT can achieve good reconstruction in a costly optimization time but often fails to produce accurate manipulation and consistent videos
  • DICoMoGAN: Due to the architecture design of DICoMoGAN that follows frame-to-frame optimization, it can achieve good reconstruction but fails to perform any manipulation without resulting in artifacts
  • Latent Transformer: We consider Latent Transformer only for sleeve length manipulation as it requires separate training for each attribute. The latent Transformer depends on the quality of the inversion and often fails to produce temporally-consistent videos.

6. Ablation Study On Model Architecture

We test our model without its most essential losses and architecture design choice. Namely.

  • Consistency loss: without consistency loss, our model exhibited more temporal inconsistency
  • Strucutre and Appearance losses: replacing Structure and appearance losses resulted in a more blurry video.
  • Latent Directions: Predicting the target latent code instead of predicting directions resulting in an obvious change in the clothing style and identity
  • Conditional Generation Model: replacing the self-attention and cross-attention modulation with a simple concatenation resulted in a less accurate motion reconstruction
